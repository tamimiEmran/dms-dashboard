1

Focus on the driver.

The old setup analysed the whole cabin: empty passenger seat, windshield, dashboard. We now crop the picture to just the driver's area, so the AI stops getting distracted by irrelevant parts of the frame.

2

Brighten the shadows.

Cabins are often dimly lit, especially around the driver's lap and feet. A simple contrast tweak lifts the dim areas without washing out the bright windshield. The driver becomes easier to recognise.

3

Add a fallback for eating.

The food detector kept missing because food gets hidden behind a hand. We added a backup signal: if the driver's eyes are lowered and head is tilted down, that's probably eating even when the food itself is out of sight. It catches frames the detector previously gave up on.

4

Demand stronger evidence for distracted and fatigue.

Old rules fired on a single weak cue: any glance down triggered drowsy, any head turn triggered distracted. We tightened both. Distracted now requires head and eyes to agree; fatigue only fires when eyes are clearly closed, not just glancing down.

5

Net effect.

The eating use case used to be wrong 4 times out of 5. It is now correct nearly every frame. Distracted driving and fatigue both jumped once we stopped firing on weak single cues. Seatbelt was already strong, so we left it on its original setup.

Across all five use cases combined, we went from trailing the camera vendor's own model by 2.3 points to beating it by 8.0.
