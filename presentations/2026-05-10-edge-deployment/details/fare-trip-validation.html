Passenger detection — Phase 1

Camera vs. seat-sensor baseline · vehicle #3091 · ~3.5 km fixed route (HQ)

Phase 1 compares a camera against the seat-sensor baseline for detecting passenger occupancy. Black trips were run on vehicle #3091 over a fixed ~3.5 km route inside HQ, across three seating positions, recording each trip's duration, distance and fare; a stopwatch separately timed passenger-entry to trip-end. Accuracy, best to worst: Front > Rear-Right > Rear-Left.

TestedMethod & setup

A camera-based passenger detector is compared against the vehicle's seat sensor (treated as ground truth). For each black trip a single passenger sits in one position; the camera's trip readings are compared to a stopwatch and to the seat-sensor baseline. Differences in time and distance indicate how well the camera detects occupancy.

  • Seat sensor = baseline, treated as most accurate for passenger detection.
  • Time & distance differences vs. baseline are the detection-performance signal.
  • Worst case tested: 1 passenger. More passengers would raise the chance a trip starts.
  • Trip readings come from the meter app (rounding error) — Mursaal is authoritative; these are initial results.

Error analysisBy seat position
1 · Best

Front seat

Tracks the baseline closely (Δtime 35–50 s). The one large distance gap was an app shutdown, not a detection miss.

2 · Variable

Rear-Right seat

Works when contrast is good (Δtime 26 s, white shirt), but a low-contrast shirt blended with the seat and pushed detection ~3 min late.

3 · Worst

Rear-Left seat

Passenger never detected — the trip did not start at all. Highest-priority gap.

Failure modes: rear-seat coverage (especially Rear-Left), sensitivity to clothing/seat contrast, and an app stability issue (mid-trip shutdown). Front-seat detection is production-close.
